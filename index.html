An Introduction

Rock is a vast genre of music that goes back decades and has a long history. It's amazing to think about how many subgenres of Rock there are and how they came to be. Rock has been evolving since the mid-20th century and has shown up as many different genres. There have been many different supergenres that encompass a lot of subgenres. This informational site will talk about a lot the more popular genres through all of rock history.

Explore each subgenre and find out about its history as well as some notable bands from it. Each subgenre is listed under its parent genre and is in order of when it originated. From Surf Rock to Pop Punk and from Progressive Rock to Shoegaze, a lot of subgenres are organized here to read. Credits are cited on the credits page in the top right.

Rockabilly (1953)

Rock 'n' Roll and Rockabilly were not a very great beginning in terms of music. They were based on racial hatred because of how they took music from black musicians and rebranded it by having young, attractive, white singers bring it into the lives of white listeners. But that didn't stop Rock 'n' Roll from being an important step in the history of Rock. Being very similar to early R&B, it had a very uptempo blues sound with three chords and the simple intrument trinity of rock: drums, guitar and bass. White teens went crazy over this music and Rock 'n' Roll became so popular around the world that it became a term for anything rebellious, sometimes even Rock music itself.

Notable Artists:
- Elvis Presley
- Jerry Lee Lewis
- Sam Phillips

Folk Revival (1957)

After World War II, Folk music was revived for the second time after the first revival during the thirties which was suppressed by the popularity of Blues, Jazz, and R&B. The second revival was much larger as the arrival of Rock in the US and the UK let college students and bohumians use instruments to write folk-like protest songs. These songs were long and stretched out with a very distinctive chorus. They were very simple and soft acoustic songs that was more about the message then the actual musical substance. New York was the central hub for Folk Revival because of it's extensive coffee bar culture.

Notable Artists and Bands:
- Pete Seeger
- Joan Baez
- The Weavers

Surf Rock (1958)

The calm and carefree atmosphere of the beach and its lifestyle were perfect for surfers to be able to play guitar in their beach houses. They created a washing sound using incredibly fast guitar riffs that were heavily drenched in reverb that imitated ocean waves to impress girls. Almost all of Surf Rock was just instrumental with little to none lyrics which drew attention to how well the guitarists played. Surf Rock that did include lyrics was generally slower and had less technique but was still very popular, being known as Surf Pop (The Beach Boys are great example of this genre). Surf Rock had influences from Cuban malague√±a songs which made them fast and addictive. The music of Surf Rock was completely non-commercial and fun because almost all its bands cared little for promotion of their music. Unfortunately, this led to Surf Rock to become completely extinct by the age of Classic Rock.

Notable Bands:
- Dick Dale & His Del-Tones
- The Beach Boys
- The Surfaris

Garage Rock (1960)

Rockabilly was dead when all of it's main icons were either dead or in jail. However, Rock was not dead at this point because of the countless ambitious teens that were in love with Rock. They began forming their own Rock bands in their garages (hence the term Garage Rock). This resulted in a very raw and amateur sound of Rock 'n' Roll and the first uses of distorted guitars. Garage Rock is associated with the "fuzz" distortion effect because of it's extensive use of it which gave guitars a fuzzy sound. This distortion fits nicely with the content of Garage Rock: the anger and frustration of dealing with heartbreak and other negative love experiences. Garage Rock's teenage attitude of "screw-you-we-do-what-we-want" inspired Punk Rock as well as 21st century artists who felt that a revival of Garage Rock was deserved.

Notable Bands:
- The Sonics
- The Castaways
- The Wailers

British Invasion (1963)

While the rock scene in London was mostly based around Blues, the city of Liverpool was more interested in music such as Motown and American R&B. This was because the city was a large harbor city with many visitors from ocean liners. Soon, young and ambitious Rock bands began making a new type of Rock called Mersey Beat (called after the river Mersey in Liverpool) that mixed features from Motown and Rock 'n' Roll. This kicked off the second great revolution of Rock and one of Rock's most popular band that is still loved to this day, The Beatles. Many Mersey Beat bands began crossing over the ocean to 'invade' America which made the genre become commonly known as the British Invasion.

Notable Bands:
- The Beatles
- The Kinks
- The Animals

Psychedelia (1965)

The sixties marked a new age of hope, reconstruction and peace after the effects of World War II. A large hippie subculture flourished all over the country, especially places with a large student population. These young hippies were amazed by eastern cultures, music, and countless psychedelic drugs (most notably LSD with its colorful hallucinations). All of these factors were inspirations that were mixed with the most popular college music of the time: Folk Revival, Mersey Beat and Garage Rock to form an exotic and experimental style of Rock that utilized psychedelic sound effects. Many subgenres of this style became popular and were all put under the tag "Psychedelia". This genre has undergone many revivals recently such as Neo-Psychedelia which prove the popularity of it (and some of the bands that have come out of these revivals are personally my favorite bands of all time).

Notable Bands:
- Jefferson Airplane
- The 13th Floor Elevators
- The Doors

Folk Rock (1965)

The British Invasion was so popular throughout the music industry that it was basically impossible to ignore in the UK. Even small folk communities were intrigued by the strong backbeat and catchy hooks of the genre and they began adapting these elements into their music while keeping their important messages in the lyrics. This allowed the bands to sign record deals and reach a larger audience with their music, which appealed many artists who believed that the story in music was important. The first band to do this were The Byrds who began as a standard Folk band but evolved into a Folk Rock band, which helped bring many other bands along into Folk Rock.

Notable Bands & Artists:
- The Byrds
- Bob Dylan
- The Turtles

Hard Rock (1968)

In the UK, many music genres reformed in rougher "British" versions of themselves. One of which was Blues Rock from the late sixties which became Hard Rock. Radio stations needed a heavy and alternative style of music after the fall of UK's pirate radios and Hard Rock became the perfect genre to fill the gap. Hard Rock was essentially a louder and faster kind of Blues Rock which made it the first "heavy" music genre to exist, even before Heavy Metal existed. It utilized the same basic Classic Rock instrument set-up but which a lot more distortion on the guitars and simpler song structures. The "Riff", a short unique and repetitive melody which is a staple of Rock, was introduced in Hard Rock.

Notable Bands:
- ACDC
- Aerosmith
- The Who

Progressive Rock (1969)

The release of new 33rpm long-play records pushed away older and shorter 78rpm records by the end of the sixties which allowed for longer playtime on a record of up to 22 minutes per side instead of 5 minutes. This shifted the focus of many bands from creating hit singles to full-length albums, which also allowed for experimentation of song structure and progression (hence the name, Progressive Rock). The crossover nature of Psychedelia also inspired Progressive Rock artists to experiment with elements from classical music which resulted in softer, longer and more intricate songs. It was difficult to write this kind of music but also hard to perform live, but it was perfect for vinyl LPs. Also, the appearance of hi-fideelity speakers and home amplifiers further boosted the popularity of Progressive Rock.

Notable Bands:
- Pink Floyd
- King Crimson
- Supertramp

Southern Rock (1971)

After the Civil War, the South got an urge to show its pride and express itself in the form of music. Southern Rock is a mix of many of different genres including Delta (southern American Blues), Outlaw (southern American Country) and Rock 'n' Roll. Being completely patriotic, it has a very raw and loud soung with electric slide guitars and usually having multiple soloing guitars. Southern Rock can have either gentle Country songs with a touch of Blues Rock or a frantic rough rollercoaster looping and spinning in its tracks.

Notable Bands:
- Lynyrd Skynyrd
- The Allman Brothers
- Creedence Clearwater Revival

Glam Rock (1971)

With the fall of the flower power age, there came an interest towards individualism. The introduction of popular music on television also sparked this change. Some Rock artists took this opportunity to stand out with make-up and fashion to make eccentric alter egos. Glam Rock was based on the usage of visuals and make-up to give a glamorous impression while the music tended to range from Pop to Progressive Rock and sometimes Hard Rock. Glam Rock was all about the fame more than the music. Glam Rock also existed around the same time as Proto Punk, which caused the transfer of the glamour of Glam Rock to the world of Punk Rock.

Notable Bands and Artists:
- David Bowie
- Queen
- Lou Reed

A.O.R. (1974)

When people think about Rock of the mid-seventies, it's usually not Hard Rock but it's counterpart A.O.R. (short for Adult Oriented Rock). A.O.R. was a lot more alternative and mature with dramatic guitar chords and glossy production than Hard Rock, leading it to be called Soft Rock (though usually Soft Rock was just a quieter approach to A.O.R. with less solos). A.O.R. also had other names such as American Rock, Arena Rock and Stadium Rock. Much like Progressive Rock, A.O.R. bands focused on albums as a whole instead of hit singles but A.O.R. was generally a lot more catchy. The genre's success generally lies in the desire to forget all negative aspects of the early seventies and that was how it became extremely popular.

Notable Bands:
- Survivor
- Toto
- REO Speedwagon

Proto Punk (1971)

Proto Punk is a precursor to Punk Rock that is very vaquely defined, it's instead just a collection of highly influential bands and artists who can't really be placed in any other genres. These artists had a huge impace on Punk Rock and they were one of the most important Rock artists of all time.

Notable Bands:

- Velvet Underground
- Ducks DeLuxe

Punk Rock (1975)

Punk Rock was probably the biggest revolution in all of Rock history because of the economic and political situation in Britain during the early seventies. This situation made a few young delinquent kids ("punks") really angry. In the states, they rediscovered the forgotten Garage Rock mentality but another contributor for Punk Rock was the backlash to "Stadium Rock" of the early seventies. The youth were against how A.O.R. and Progressive Rock were far from the roots of Rock yet were so popular, and this idea spawned Punk Rock. The music was short, fast, energetic and raw like Hard Rock and Garage Rock, yet Punk was much more intense. Punk had a very provocative culture with extreme haircuts, rebellious fashion, and disgustinbg behavior, and this complimented Punk Rock greatly.

Notable Bands:
- The Clash
- The Ramones
- The Damned

Anarcho-Punk (1977)

As well as being music, Punk Rock was also a marketing machine, cleverly manipulated by the movement's frontman Malcom Mclaren. In reaction to this, a new subgenre was formed by the name Anarcho-Punk. Bands from this style swore by a heavy DIY approach and had little to no marketing or publicity. This became a prelude to Hardcore Punk. Also, Anarcho-Punk included real political messages, fueled by the shallowness of popular Punk Rock bands' lyrics. Anarcho-Punk was later influenced by Extreme Metal around 1985 and adopted a more chaotic and faster sound.

Notable Bands:
- Crass
- Subhumans
- Antisect

Post-Punk (1978)

Both Punk Rock and the Punk movement died around the same time Disco was gaining popularity. This casted a gloomy shadow of both fear and hate among Rock artists and bands. Immediated after Punk died, Post-Punk has risen, being a form of Punk Rock without its edges and naivety. A dark struggle is noticeable in Post-Punk's music, showing a difficulty of coping with what society has become. Post-Punk had a lot longer and more complex songs than Punk but they also had repetitive elements to induce a kind of trance. Post-Punk didn't really have a unified sound however due to it's experimental nature, but New Wave was there to change that.

Notable Bands:
- Joy Division
- The Psychedelic Furs
- The Fall

Hardcore Punk

Originated as a subgenre of Punk, it grew quickly in both the US and the UK. It was originally defined by a lifestyle that is more disciplined and pure but the average perception of Hardcore was a lot more negative because of the aggressive nature of the music. Hardcore has lasted decades from mottos in its community that are along the lines of "Hardcore Will Never Die" and "Forever Hardcore". Being mostly underground, it has lived on longer than any other genre here.

Alternative Rock

After the mid-eighties, Rock couldn't really be considered mainstream and Alternative Rock stemmed from that. It dominated the Rock scene from the mid-eighties to the end of the nineties. It is a collection of a lot of different styles but is usually recognizable by lots of distortion and sentimental lyrics. Songs are longer and slower than other Rock genres with the exception of progressive rock. Alternative Rock died with the end of the millenium but lives on in the form of Indie Rock.

These genres were formed from taking old genres and forming completely new ones out of them. Contemporary Rock is defined by the building of bridges to other musical genres.
